Detection classes

7 critical safety objects, one unified model

ARIES identifies every major safety asset aboard a space station in a single inference pass. Each class was selected based on ISS emergency protocols and NASA safety documentation, ensuring real-world operational relevance beyond the challenge.

The model learns to distinguish objects across wildly varying contexts — from a fire extinguisher mounted flush against a bulkhead panel to an oxygen tank floating freely in microgravity. Confidence scores above 92% across all classes underscore the robustness of the synthetic training approach.

Oxygen Tank
Pressurized O₂ cylinders used for life support and EVA operations aboard the station. Critical for crew survival in any depressurization event or emergency oxygen top-up scenario.
Avg conf 97.2%
Nitrogen Tank
Inert gas storage used in pressurization systems and thruster propellant management. Distinguished from oxygen tanks by label markings and body geometry variations visible even at low resolution.
Avg conf 94.8%
First Aid Box
Emergency medical kits containing supplies for injury, burns, and decompression incidents. White housing with prominent red cross marking enables high-confidence detection even in partial occlusion.
Avg conf 95.6%
Fire Alarm
Smoke and heat triggered alarm panels that alert crew to fire or smoke anomalies in any module. Panel-mounted units vary in form factor across modules; the model handles all known ISS variants.
Avg conf 96.1%
Safety Switch Panel
Emergency shutdown and override panels controlling critical station systems and power circuits. Identification is time-sensitive in any cascade failure scenario aboard a pressurized habitat.
Avg conf 93.4%
Emergency Phone
Hardwired communication terminals for direct contact with ground control during emergencies. Unlike standard handheld devices, these are fixed-mount units with a distinctive housing shape and indicator lights.
Avg conf 92.9%
Fire Extinguisher
CO₂ and dry-chemical suppression units mounted throughout crew quarters and lab modules. The model distinguishes between handheld and rack-mounted variants through body proportion analysis. Orientation-invariant detection ensures reliable identification in microgravity where objects may be mounted in non-standard positions, including upside-down or at oblique angles relative to the camera frame.
Avg conf 95.3%
End-to-end workflow

From synthetic data to real detection

The full pipeline runs in four discrete stages — from photorealistic simulation all the way through to benchmarked inference. Every step is reproducible and documented for open submission review.

Synthetic-to-real transfer is at the heart of this approach. By generating unlimited labeled training data inside a physics simulator, we bypass the laborious and expensive process of on-orbit data collection — which currently does not exist at the scale needed for robust deep learning.

01
DATA GENERATION
Synthetic Dataset via Falcon
Rendered inside Duality AI's Falcon simulator with physically accurate lighting, occlusions, depth-of-field blur, and camera angle variation across a complete ISS-inspired space station scene. No real labeled images needed — all ground-truth annotations are generated programmatically at render time, enabling rapid scaling to tens of thousands of frames with zero human effort.
02
PREPARATION
Environment Setup
Clone the repository and activate the provided EDU conda environment. All dependencies, CUDA drivers, and dataset paths are pre-configured for full reproducibility. A single setup script validates the environment, downloads dataset splits from the challenge bucket, and runs a smoke test inference to confirm the pipeline is ready. Works on both local GPU rigs and cloud notebooks.
conda activate edu && pip install -r requirements.txt
03
TRAINING
YOLO Fine-Tuning
Fine-tune YOLOv8 on the synthetic dataset with configurable epochs, batch size, and augmentation settings. Supports multi-GPU training and mixed-precision (FP16) for faster convergence. Mosaic augmentation, random flipping, HSV jitter, and copy-paste augmentation are all applied during training to maximize synthetic diversity and improve real-world generalization from day one.
python train.py --epochs 100 --batch 16
04
EVALUATION
Benchmark and Predict
Run evaluation to get full metrics: mAP@0.5, precision–recall curves, confusion matrix, and per-class F1 scores. Visualize predictions on held-out test images with color-coded bounding boxes. WandB integration provides real-time training dashboards and automatic artifact versioning, making it easy to compare runs and submit the best checkpoint to the leaderboard.
python predict.py --weights best.pt
Model performance

Benchmarked across all conditions

All metrics are evaluated on the held-out synthetic validation split using standard COCO evaluation protocols. The model was never exposed to the test set during any phase of training or hyperparameter selection.

Performance is consistently strong across all 7 classes, with no single class acting as a significant outlier — a direct result of balanced class sampling in the Falcon rendering pipeline and targeted augmentation strategies applied to underrepresented poses during training.

Synthetic training data

Built on photorealistic simulation

The entire training dataset is generated using Duality AI's Falcon simulator — a high-fidelity physics-based rendering engine that produces photorealistic images of the space station environment without requiring physical hardware or on-orbit data collection.

Every image is automatically annotated with precise bounding box labels, eliminating the need for manual labeling. The simulator varies lighting intensity, shadow direction, camera pose, object placement, and occlusion levels across thousands of frames to ensure robust generalization across the full range of real operational conditions.

This approach lets us build a fully supervised detection model entirely from synthetic data, then deploy it in real environments — demonstrating the power of sim-to-real transfer for safety-critical applications where real training data is impossible to collect at scale.

Domain randomization techniques were applied throughout the rendering process: surface textures are varied per frame, lens aberrations are simulated, motion blur from camera movement is introduced stochastically, and object scale variations reflect the range of actual hardware found across ISS modules from the US segment to the Russian Orbital Segment.

The final dataset contains over 18,000 rendered frames split into training, validation, and test subsets at a 75 / 15 / 10 ratio. Class balance is enforced at the scene-composition level, ensuring no single object category dominates and that the model cannot exploit frequency shortcuts during training.

All annotations conform to the YOLO label format with normalized bounding box coordinates, making the dataset immediately compatible with the full Ultralytics ecosystem and any other detection framework that accepts standard COCO-style inputs.

Dynamic Lighting Conditions
From harsh direct sunlight through portholes to near-zero lux emergency scenarios — 12 distinct lighting configurations including LEDs, fluorescents, and orbital sunrise flare.
Multi-Angle Camera Poses
360-degree camera coverage simulating mounted ceiling cameras, handheld inspection cameras, and robotic arm cameras across all six station modules.
Randomized Occlusion Scenarios
Objects are partially hidden behind cables, equipment panels, crew members, and storage nets to prevent overfitting to clean, unoccluded views.
Auto-Generated YOLO Annotations
Every bounding box is programmatically generated with zero human labeling, enabling rapid dataset scaling to tens of thousands of precisely labeled images without annotation fatigue or inconsistency.
Technology stack

Built with modern, open-source tools

The pipeline leverages a carefully chosen set of open-source tools and cloud infrastructure to ensure fast training cycles, full reproducibility, and straightforward evaluation submission. Every component was selected for production readiness, not just benchmark performance.

YOLOv8 from Ultralytics forms the detection backbone, chosen for its state-of-the-art accuracy-speed trade-off and extensive documentation. PyTorch 2.x with CUDA 12 enables mixed-precision training that cuts memory usage in half without measurable accuracy loss. OpenCV handles all preprocessing and visualization pipelines, while WandB provides experiment tracking and artifact versioning across every training run.

The Duality Falcon simulator interfaces directly with Python via a headless rendering API, allowing fully automated dataset generation without manual scene setup. All randomization parameters — lighting, camera intrinsics, object placement, and occlusion density — are driven by a reproducible seed file committed alongside the model weights, ensuring every training dataset can be regenerated from scratch for verification purposes.
